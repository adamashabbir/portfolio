    DEVELOP & MONETISE YOUR DIGITAL PRESENCE

    TAKE CARE & SHARE

    Develop & Monetise your Digital Presence.
    I believe our digital existence is separate from our offline presence. How we present ourselves online and whom we connect with on connected devices differs from real life collisions. This is the beauty of developing your digital presence; it is an opportunity to experiment, connect with new communities and escape from your offline routine.

    I am keen to share my main learning’s from developing my digital presence, specifically through my online portfolio. I hope you find them useful.

    Swift introduction to myself to give context; I studied at Oxford University and New York University and have founded two startups. It’s not all play; in my spare time I run ultra marathons and am currently training for the worlds toughest footrace. Within the tech industry I am what’s known as a “Full Stack” product developer and designer. Firstly I want to express I am fairly new to software development having previously explored UX & hardware. I am far from a tiptop developer. However, I have taught myself and have thankfully been guided by some great online and offline communities in London and NYC.

    Hardware to Software Before I discuss why having a digital presence has helped me and can help you. I want to express why I switched from hardware to software. I became frustrated with hardware’s elongated feedback loop. I am obsessed with big, real-time data. For me, it should influence user-led, iterative design. Moreover, it is inspiring for development teams to see, in the studio, real time metrics of user engagement and overall application performance; it injects confidence into every product team. Obviously there is real-time data in hardware, however because continuous integration is impossible due its physical nature, it is difficult to rapidly iterate.

    Unlike software, due to its physicality hardware deployment tends to take the form of “big bang” releases, which subsequently means the product is already 6 months old the day it is deployed. Depending on your product team, this can also be the case in software, which is why building your own online portfolio gives you the opportunity to explore iterative development.

    Prior to pushing the product over the line, growth hacking and user testing hardware platforms is very costly and time consuming. Getting users geographically present alone can be a nightmare. Additionally a swift search on jobsite.co.uk demonstrates the excess demand for software developers compared to hardware. With wearable technology on the rise, this may not be the case forever as the two mediums begin to intertwine. But for now, I have fallen in love with the rapid nature of software development, its global user base and helpful communities.

    Lets dive into the benefits of developing your digital presence with an online portfolio. User testing has shown that for growth hacking and marketing reasons, ‘portfolio’ has more gravitas and maturity than website. From now on, instead of talking about your ‘website’ share it as your ‘portfolio’. This is subjective so feel free to call it your ‘web app’ if you fancy, just don’t expect much engagement.

    At the core, your portfolio is an exhibition of your interests, funneled into one domain. Documenting these interests does not always have to be text based. A portfolio should merge multiple channels. For instance my portfolio showcases my inventions, I host a podcast and blog about life in startups and on start lines. The reason for this is simple. The variety of medium retains users and encourages them to explore instead of bounce off.

    BE MY GUEST I encourage you to think creatively about which mediums to merge on your portfolio, but its useful to have more than one. Based on data, audio, text and video work nicely for me; it doesn’t place all the pressure on you. Guest podcasts and blog posts ensure your content is fresh and frequent. Additionally, guests are free marketing tools driving traffic to your platform through their network. Their experience and opinions also teach you far more than you can teach yourself. The favor works both ways, so requesting to be a guest blogger or podcaster is something I actively do. Furthermore, these guests are the foundations of your online community. Yes, they are your first school friends who will be there with you through thick and thin so treat them, as you would wish to be treated.

    Don’t be rude, include. Learning on the job is obviously a must. As developers, to keep up with modern dev practices you should be constantly challenging yourself at work. However, depending on the product team or client request, your tech stack can be limiting and frustrating. For this reason, developing your own portfolio is essential. An absolute must actually. It allows you to explore new frameworks and excel whilst developing something you enjoy. It is an opportunity to dive into what you adore doing, permitting you to push the boundaries of your digital and technical knowledge.

    Passion As well as technical self-development, developing your online portfolio gives you self-confidence. Rarely in life do we openly discuss our achievements, aspirations and interests. We definitely don’t amongst family and friends because they should already know you and it would be plain odd. Also, discussing such things in work would just be weird. The only time I tend to do this is offline during interviews. Even then, due to interview infrequency I usually don’t phrase my learning’s correctly and forget a lot of what I have done. Your portfolio is a portal to express yourself and host all of the above. Everyday, as developers and designers we are building something and usually collaborating on the go. Sadly, our brains are not cloud storage; we cannot remember every product input. However, your online portfolio is essentially personalized, open source cloud storage, styled how you wish. Taking the time to jot all everyday learning’s down has benefited me massively; it shapes my opinion on the subject at hand, ensures I don’t forget what I built three years ago and within a working environment allows me to maturely express myself. The sooner you build your digital presence the better. Everyone says this but it is true. Build it, learn from it and iterate it.

    Build yourself Building your portfolio is, then, in effect building yourself. It is also a very fun activity. Just like facebook flashes up with memories, flicking back through your portfolio over the years should be like opening an old school book. You will giggle at some of your early content and framework choices. But remember, those experiences have helped shape you so don’t be embarrassed. My best boss to date taught me the importance of leaving digital breadcrumbs for others to gobble up. Your digital presence should be an ongoing project that leads others to you.

    When blogging, although it is useful self-documentation, consider how your experiences can help someone else. You should aim to create fresh content that helps to positively influence others. With podcasts, a good base is to create a conversation with your guest that can be continued by your listeners. Remember to make them intimate, deep and often intense; these are not interviews. They are collaborative conversations that aim to inspire, provoke and challenge your listeners.

    Job Options Another huge benefit of having a dominant digital presence is job opportunities and differentiating yourself from the crowd. External learning, development and documentation exhibit passion. Passion is something every company wants at their heart. Since developing and deploying my online portfolio I have received job opportunities at least once a day. This is not for everyone, but I think it is beneficial to know the state of the market. Furthermore, this comes back to confidence; it demonstrates you are in demand and removes the risk from testing the job market yourself.

    If you are, like me, interested in startup culture and product development then building your digital presence is the perfect introduction to concepts of growth hacking and monetization. I have built a lot of personal projects. Some for fun, others I seriously believe in. However, maintaining constant concentration and growing a user base for these is tricky. It requires immense self-discipline, determination and time.

    The splendor of your online portfolio is it is you. It is your online presence and therefore, you are contracted to improve and grow it. In order to do this you must learn, for yourself, how to successfully growth hack your digital presence. I am writing a separate piece on how to do this effectively and efficiently at a low cost; so do check back for that one. Growth hacking my portfolio has been immensely useful; my budget is my own and I am not restricted by brand rules on growth and monetization. I now know how to share through the right channels and have become addicted to engaging, maintaining and growing my portfolios user base. To prove I am not fluffing, check out the data from this past week. It is far from viral but it demonstrates how consistent connection with your community is crucial. As well as this, the next time you have a web app idea, host the landing page on your portfolio and get concept feedback through your portfolio. Spreading yourself too thinly with numerous .io or .co domains is very demanding, especially if you are an extrepreneur and have a full time job which I do. This is why I share all of my products through my portfolio and then develop them further if feedback and beta testing goes well. For instance, Common Pence began on my portfolio and following positive feedback and user testing; it evolved to live separately at commonpence.co

    Analytics Placing analytics on your portfolio is a must; it ensures you are up to speed with your users. As well as this, it gets you hooked on your own platforms success. Given the ‘always on, always connected’ era we exist in, you can check anywhere how many users are present, where users are spending time and where they are not. This real-time data has the ‘notification’ effect i.e you grow a desire to drive more users to your platform and improve upon yesterdays engagement. Many product teams leave analytics till the end. For me, the sooner you implement them the better. I opt for Google Analytics; their UI and mobile app is a thrill.

    Monetize wise A few of your friends may take the mick to begin but if you opt to monetize your portfolio they will soon become very quiet. If executed properly, your digital existence can generate more than a typical offline job. The most successful platforms out there generate £100,000+ a year. In order to reach these heights you have to start somewhere, but remember to start. Importantly, don’t expect millions in minutes.

    My online portfolio has given me a core understanding of monetization techniques. I am not claiming to write a monetization manual, but my learning’s may help a few people.

    Once you are confident in traffic I encourage everyone to experiment with Google Adsense. On my platform, on average, each advert at the base of every page roughly makes £1.08 per click and sometimes a click can convert to £3.22. This is not much as a one off, but as traffic increases, this can become lucrative. Obviously, within tech, we spot adverts easily. However, amongst our community, if people enjoyed a post they often show their love by clicking one of these adverts; they know every little helps. Last month my platform made £768.09 and it is predicted to make close to £8500 this year. Hopefully, this amount should only increase year on year as your community grows inline with your content.

    Ebook Other monetization routes to explore are Single Kindle e-books, which have a huge success if executed properly with proper pre-ordering etc. This option should be explored when you are 6 months in and have a core following. I am dyslexic so this has taken me a little longer to ship. But I am releasing my first e-book in January – Learn to Code in 20 seconds. I shall document the process here so do check back.

    On a personal note, I have been exploring product placement with my ads page. However this requires a lot more care and curating, but can be very profitable if you find the right product. Monetizing your portfolio is a personal venture that has to be adapted to your audience. An offline monetization route that has grown from my portfolio is a chilled ‘Code Lounge’ that I run each Monday evening from my living room. This may not be for everyone but I find it a great practice and have met some cool people running it. The course has four sessions, each one costing £100. Every session has 5 folks so each month code lounge generates £2000. This is shifting your online existence offline but the key is sharing knowledge with a targeted audience. For instance, ‘Code Lounge’ teaches working professionals how to code and the core fundamentals of joining startups. The course would not have gained gravitas if I hadn’t have built my digital presence.

    That’s a wrap To conclude I have learnt a lot from building my own online portfolio. It has allowed me to experiment with a variety of frameworks; user test specific UI and most importantly I have met some amazing people along the way. I encourage everyone to give it a go and please share your feedback and learning’s in the comments below. Thanks for taking the time to read this.
